tornait May 24, 2018, 5:18am #135

I was a heavy skeptic of this pack at first. I thought, “Ugh, these leads. I can’t make any of these into good chord sounds…” Until I realized that they weren’t supposed to be chord sounds. However, this pack grew on me. Instead of leads, I treat them as basses. They work pretty well for basses, especially Mental, which might overtake Pasta and Blend as my favorite bass sound, even though it’s a lead. I will say this, however; I’m happy that the Auxy team is catering towards a vide variety of genres with these packs, but I feel that this one doesn’t have as wide of a reach as all of the previous ones. Because of the distortion in the sounds, none of them can sound good as chords, regardless of how much automation is done. This saddens me, as I’m always looking to fatten up my songs with thick chords.

Finally, I will express my dissatisfaction with the fact that there is no chiptune pack yet. However, the month of June is my birth month. I’ll be praying and praying that a personal miracle comes my way in the form of a sound pack which would be more favorable to my style of music. That aside, quality sounds— Oh, and classic Auxy names for the sounds. I’d say a solid 7/10 for me. Good quality sounds, though the lack of compatibility with chord creation knocks off a point or two in my book.

P.S. Fuze is now one of my go to leads, and Cone reminds me so much of a distorted guitar-like lead.

Oh boy

So, Riddim is a subgenre of Dubstep, and derives from Jamaican Reggae (someone correct me if I’m wrong). I guess modern music took over reggae and converted it to a new style of dubstep called Riddim.

The genre isn’t as popular as genres like Future Bass or EDM, but a select few of producers have kept it going. For example, people like Oolacile, MuK and INFEKT.

The main difference with dubstep is that Riddim is more repitive in terms of drums and it has a more grittier feel to it, as well as a bit more bouncy, much like the sort of jamaican bounce that you’d hear, hence it’s origin.

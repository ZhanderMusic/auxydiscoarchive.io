Stereo information is not really about left and right, it is the difference between the left and right. Confusing right? Here is the easiest way to imagine it: you have a hotdog in front of you. The sausage is all the mono information that comes out of both speakers equally‚Äîvocals, kick, bass, most percussion etc. The bun is the sides. This is the information spread between left and right. The sausage should always be level with or above the top of the bread. If the bread is higher and the sausage is sinking into the bread, you have a really sh*t hotdog, or in audio terms, you have phasing.

Phasing (aka flanging - per analog tape) is adding the same signal to itself, offset by a tiny delay (the phase angle), which is varied. You get a comb filtering effect where certain frequencies cancel according to what the delay is at a given time. It‚Äôs easy to picture when you think of summing two sine waves that are offset by half a wavelength: one‚Äôs peak coincides with the other‚Äôs trough, and they cancel. Every sound is just a sum of of sine waves at different frequencies, so they take turns cancelling when you vary the delay. When the levels are matched, you‚Äôll get the maximum effect, but you are correct that it‚Äôs more about the timing.

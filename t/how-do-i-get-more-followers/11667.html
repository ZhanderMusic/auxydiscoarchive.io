How Do I Get Real Followers?
Sunny August 12, 2018, 10:12am #2

Comment, participate in forums, promote your work, follow a lot of people, and follow back when they follow you. In order to create a successful following you can either be very active in the community, or you can prove your worth by alternate content (i.e. YouTube videos, vlogs, Instagram). And when all else fails, promote your music commercially.

I personally detest anything other than being active in the community, and working for every follower, but making money and being successful is generally a numbers game. When you fall down that rabbit hole of researching how to do it, it’ll become clear.

What’s the most important thing, whatever your method, is make sure you’re producing professional content to begin with, always upsell yourself, and be regular with your releases. Even I cannot contend with the fact that these are good habits to keep.

Sunny August 12, 2018, 11:34am #6

You are very young, so it will be difficult to get people to take you seriously on YouTube. I’m in my 20’s, trying to get 40 year olds trying to take me seriously so they’ll give me money.

Still, if you approach your craft with enough enthusiasm and consistent passion, you will do fine. Just always look to learn and grow. You’ll be approached with a lot of criticism over the next couple of years, as the praise you recieved for being “hey, that’s pretty good for someone your age!” will eventually turn into “yeah, your stuff is ok” if you don’t seek to push the limits of your understanding.

Be your best critic, but also consistently seek criticism, and act on criticism to improve.

I do not wish to discourage you from a YouTube vlog, as it is good practise, but do not expect a big following there either. There are forces that work against you there too, but it’s all about creating content and getting your work known.

Also, do not hesitate to share your music with your friends and family.

Arimyth August 12, 2018, 9:21pm #13

This question has been asked so many times on this Discourse forum alone and the answer is always the same.

  1. Build yourself an engaged audience. Go interact with other artists, do repost trades, talk to people and get referrals from people bigger than yourself. People don’t come to you in the music industry or pretty much any industry, so it’s up to you to take the initiative. Extend the kind of support that you expect to be reciprocated to you.

  2. Stick to a more fixed track schedule. This is more a strategy for keeping your current followers engaged, but your track dates are all over the place on your profile which doesn’t make for a reliable source of music. Even if it means holding off posting a track for a couple days, it’s better than posting 3 in a 2-day window and then being absent for a month. A real life example is Southborne–he doesn’t post any of his tracks here anymore but I still listen to all of them because I know to check his profile approximately once every month for a new track.

  3. Use real promotional methods to showcase your tracks. For the record, posting your tracks in the “Finished Tracks” category on the disco does not count. You need to be seeking out feedback streams, finding repost trade groups, looking for networks, diversifying the audiences you release to, and using word of mouth at the very least.

  4. Understand where you stand. It might help to take the mindset of “my base level of what my tracks ‘deserve’ is little to nothing. Why would people find me if I don’t seek them out? What makes my tracks so special as opposed to the millions of other producers out there that promote for hours or days on end after they release a track?” While that won’t directly stimulate growth, it’ll give you a better perspective on what you can expect given the work that you put into it (be it large or small).

  5. Gauge your level of commitment to music. The majority of so called “Auxy musicians” are hobbyists. They produce music in their spare time on their train commute or during their lunch break and casually promote to a few of their friends or possibly post it to a couple forums. That’s completely fine, but the expected payout is different to a full(er) time musician who dedicated portions of his day to not only producing but engaging in the promotional tactics I mentioned above. Find which category you fit in and take the appropriate steps to make sure you get what you put in.

  6. Build your brand. While these are generally relatively smaller things, the importance they play into creating a successful producer name is still very significant. Stick to one main logo with possible variations on it including color scheme and mask. Create social media outlets for that name to increase people’s knowledge of it. etcetera etcetera.

Honestly with all the times I see people ask this they always have something in common. They don’t promote almost at all but still expect the listeners to just roll in, or they’re just in the extreme hobbyist end of the spectrum and shouldn’t expect anything more. Or they just have an unrealistic expectation of what their music should receive without really listening to the feedback the community inherently gives them. Whatever community you release to is the best judge of what the community likes, so if they’re not listening to your stuff, that’s something for you to think about and act on.

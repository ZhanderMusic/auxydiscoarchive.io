akabillposters March 11, 2018, 8:37am #17

  1. Create movement, texture and interest in sounds using automation on Low Pass Filter, Hi Pass Filter, Shape and Tone.

  2. Layer sounds / avoid using unaltered stock Auxy sounds.

  3. Understand and make use of transitions in your compositions.

  4. Use Chromatic mode as much as possible. It opens you up to so many more possibilities.

  5. Always remember: It’s not about Auxy, it’s about your music, so also join other (non-Auxy) music communities.

  6. Actively listen to lots of music. Read lots of articles (starting here, here or here, maybe).

  7. Understand what you want to get out of making music - and set some goals.

  8. Have fun.

Maland2016 March 11, 2018, 11:36am #18

I’ve noticed that most people are talking about music itself, so I’ll do a tip for something different, because it’s also useful.

Naming songs is hard. No doubt about that. If you don’t already have a name in mind for your song, it’s good to listen to the whole thing and just let your mind go elsewhere. What do you see? What does it remind you of? Those results can make good song titles sometimes.

If that doesn’t work, simply think of the mood of the song. If it’s a bouncy upbeat tune, try something like “Good Times Today”, or maybe it’s a sad, but also electronic tune. In that case, try something like “Digital Lament”.

And if all that doesn’t work, the internet is your friend. Search up song names for the type of song you’ve made and see what comes up.

Hopefully this helps!

viiitality March 11, 2018, 3:32pm #20
  1. Learn a bit of music theory. Don’t go overboard, but learn the basics of rhythms, chords, and chord progressions.
  1. Create your own synths by layering and experimenting with filters. Try and stay away from the basic Auxy sounds. Instead, layer 2-3 different instruments, twiddle with the tone/shape/lowpass filters, and see what happens.
  1. Go ham with percussion. Toms, percussion, hats, kicks, snares. Try and experiment with the sounds you have, and set the mood for the song. Drum loops with 32nds and spazzy drums can work toward dark, fast songs, while drum loops with light drums and pitched snares can work for lighter songs (like future bass).
  1. Listen repeatedly to what you make. You might accidentally forget to add automation to an instrument, or a melody might be too loud. Listen over and over to spot any mistakes and fix it before you publish it.
  1. Accept criticism. I’ll admit, when I was young I hated criticism. But, in order to be consistently improving, you need to take advice from others, whether it be good or bad. Trust me, you will improve.

Db909 March 11, 2018, 5:23pm #21

OWN your music. Don’t be ashamed of it, even if it’s terrible, own it. Be like “hey guys listen to this monster turd I crapped out today can you believe it”?

It’s ok to disregard 90% of any production/mixing advice you receive, there are after all a bunch of ways to achieve pleasing sound, your friend may be advising you to take the most complicated and time wasting route. Like others have said EXPERIMENT. It’s your lab after all.

Now when people give you advice regarding the actual music, or different perspectives on how music works, etc, that’s some stuff to keep in your back pocket.

Remember that more often than not more tools DOES NOT equal more music made. It’s the opposite too much. Choose carefully and give yourself a small number of them to focus on so you can actually master them.

Remember that full, rich sounding recordings were at one point made with unimaginably limited equipment by today’s standards. Will that 27 band multi band compressor really be the thing that puts your music over the top? Do you need 13 layers of synths of which only 2 are actually audible? No. Compose better songs.

Learn how to master your own songs. The processes involved reveal much about, well pretty much everything production wise and even composition.

But most importantly like others have said HAVE FUN. And if you’re not switch things up. Making music should not feel like a chore and I would say that if that feeling arises, the resulting music is likely to be crap. Remember these are your own vibes being brought forth into musical form, maybe work on your own internal vibes. Take care of yourself for chrissakes

Pinewoodog March 13, 2018, 4:16am #31

My tips:

  1. Finish as many tracks as you can as fast as you can. In the beginning, you’re going to have a lot of inspiration, and it can be discouraging spending weeks on a track only to realize that the stuff you did weeks ago was trash. Creating new songs rapidly will help you learn new tricks, even though in the end only a select few of those should be published.

  2. Don’t get caught up in the details. You can read as many books on mixing or mastering as you want, but the real way to learn this stuff is to create. Going back to tip one; just make it! Otherwise you’ll just be disappointed later on by a track you spent months on.

  3. Listen to the kind of music you want to produce. Nobody can create a future bass song if they’ve never heard one in their life. So if you’re looking to make dubstep, listen to a lot of dubstep. That way, you’ll be getting more inspiration for your tracks. Also, don’t make the mistake of choosing something weird. If you like listening to trap and only listen to trap, then make trap. Chances are, your trap song is going to turn out better than your house song.

  4. Don’t obsess over details or waste time. I can’t tell you how many hours I spent searching the internet for free plugins that I never used. Try to make do with what you have. Sometimes, that can lead to even more creativity.

  5. Don’t give up. If you’re sad because your music isn’t up to par with other producers, just keep on going. You’re constantly getting better. The only thing that can stop you from getting better is yourself. And if you never stop, well then, you’ll achieve your goals.

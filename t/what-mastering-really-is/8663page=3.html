In iTunes (for computer), it gives you a master peak level for the track.
An Auxy master peaks at like -4 dB or something.
A good master peaks at -4 through like -6.
Bad masters will peak at -9 dB. Spinnin’ Records tracks usually peak at -8 or -9, which is WAY too loud. (#EndTheLoudnessWars)

Ps. iTunes reads peak levels a little different. For iTunes, the louder a track is, the the lower the number will be. So, a super quite track is +1, and a super loud track -9.

Auxy March 20, 2018, 8:45pm #59
akabillposters:

If mastering uses the same tools and fx as mixing, how/why did it become a separate process? Why hasn’t mastering been absorbed into mixing - i.e. why doesn’t the industry aim for master-level quality and characteristics during the mix stage?

Indeed! I think mastering as a specific occupation dates back to vinyl where you would have to master the track to make the most of the limited bandwidth and headroom that you had to work with. I.e. the mix engineer would work to make the mix sound as good as possible, and the mastering engineer would then work to make that mix sound as good as possible on the specific medium, e.g. vinyl.

Today, you could argue that there’s no need for a mastering engineer since the mix engineer could do the work of making the mix sound great at the mix stage. Although there might be something to the argument that someone who specializes in optimizing mixes for today’s listening conditions could be an expert at just that. On the other hand, it could also just be that people ascribe more awesome than there is to mastering engineers just out of legacy.

Traditionally…

Market and promote the artist and their music, generating greater exposure and revenue than the artist can typically achieve on their own.
Provide upfront investment to the artist, giving them an income while they develop future material and develop as artists.
Provide upfront costs for things like studio time, session artists, merch manufacturing, venue bookings (for shows), …
Bring together potential collaborators (producers, songwriters, etc…)
Handle the release and publishing of material

This is still how the larger labels function, more or less.

Smaller/independent labels still function in that model, but the sums of money for upfront costs are typically much smaller.
There’s relatively little, if any, investment in artist development, with the expectation being more about signing fully-formed artists who already have release-ready material.

In this situation, it only makes sense for artists to sign to such labels if they can multiply the audience size and multiply the revenue generated by the music.

Because labels often take a substantial % of the revenue, unsigned artists are increasingly self-releasing their music or creating their own independent label (relatively cheap and simple to do these days).

Consequently, there’s been a proliferation of small/micro-labels in recent years, making it easier than ever to be ‘signed to a label’. This means that the cache of being signed to a label isn’t what it once was - unless you’re signed to one of the majors.

 
The basic thing for artists to remember is that it only makes sense to sign to a label if the label can multiply the exposure and revenue. e.g. if the label is going to take 50% of revenue, it will need to more than double the exposure/revenue before it makes sense for the artist to sign.

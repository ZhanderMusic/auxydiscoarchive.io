Well, it’s important to understand what they are. Triplets are technically out of time- they force 3 notes into the space 4 notes normally take. The key to using triplets is making sure the phrase is really clearly using triplets and then moving back to regular notes. Example- you may have a phrase taking up beats 1&2 but in beat 3 start the beat with a triplet and fill it (so three notes) then go back to the original feel at beat 4. Don’t overuse them and it does take practice to get a feel for them!

Why are the columns labeled 1,3,5 and 7 and what does thinking about it that way do for me?
Simplifies understanding scales and keys.

One of the most useful things I learned has to do with a thing called Scale Degrees.
I learned it a long time ago, but I really “get it” now because of the way Auxy is set up. I’m sure this will help someone and will seem basic to others.

So for example:
in the C major scale the notes are
C D E F G A B
This is the 'ole Do Re Mi… etc

We can number these as scale degrees
C D E F G A B
1 2 3 4 5 6 7

This is why there are 7 columns in Auxy and what they represent

If you are in the Key of C major in settings, the columns left to right are C D E F G A B.

If we play mostly these notes in a song, it is considered to be in the key of C major.
These notes are mostly going to sound good together. The farther two notes are from each other the better they are going to sound together. For example C (the 1st degree) and G (the fifth degree) sound good together. C and D (1 and 2) at the same time doesn’t sound as harmonious C and G. But they are still in the same key so they work great in the context of a song. They do sound more uncomfortable together, not great, but still ok. C and C# (1 and 1.5 I guess) are too close together - doesn’t sound good to most people.

When auxy is not in chromatic mode, none of those .5 scale degree intervals are shown. This makes in dead simple to play “in key”.

But there are all kinds of scales!
To make a major scale into a melodic minor the rule is to make the 3rd, 6th and 7th scale degrees flat (lower pitched) by one half step.
This is always the rule, no matter with which letter note you are starting.

So the C Minor scale notes become
C D Eb F G Ab Bb

But here’s the cool thing… we can still number these as scale degrees the same way as with the major scale.
C D Eb F G Ab Bb
1 2 3 4 5 6 7
because C is the first note, D is the second, and now Eb is the third instead of E etc.

So in auxy the the third column changes from E to Eb when you change the key from C major to C minor and the columns stay 1 to 7.

You can play in any key (major or minor) without having to know what the notes in that scale are.
I don’t need to know which notes need to be flat or sharp to make a clean Do Re Mi scale when starting with any other note. Again dead simple.

The scales degrees for major scales are always: 1,2,3,4,5,6,7
For minor scales the notes are always: 1,2, flat 3,4,5, flat 6, flat 7.
But in Auxy, its simplified. Still the columns are always 1 to 7.

Now you can change the key from C to anything else that you want and it will always work.
You don’t have to know what notes make up a F# scale because the scale degrees are always 1,2,3,4,5,6,7.
And if you only play those notes in a song they will probably sound like they belong together.

Next lesson: how thinking about numbered scale degrees is magical when it comes to Chords!

Someone said that knowing math helps. It not so much math as just being able to count to 7.

Part 2 Intervals and Chords

A lot of this will seem correct or obvious if you have played with chords in Auxy much. I want to write now about why it sounds the way it does when you add up notes to make chords. Now that you understand about scale degrees.

Let’s start with talking about intervals between notes in a scale. I already mentioned about how a 1 and a 2 notes at the same time will sound less in tune with each other than a 1 and a 5. (By the way the same is true if you were comparing a 3 and a 4 to a 3 and a 7 for example) you can hear this yourself by experimenting if you haven’t already.

We call a 1 and a 5 at the same time a perfect fifth. And a 1 and a 3 is a 3rd. Notice the interval between a 1 and a 3 and a 3 and a 5 are the same. We call this stacking thirds. In this three note “stacked third” you can hear the effect of 1-3, 3-5 and 1-5 all at the same time.

Any three or more notes at the same time is called chord and a chord with three notes is often called a triad.

A 1,3,5 chord is called a major or minor chord depending on whether the 3rd scale degree is from a major or minor chord. The chord is named after the name of the 1st degree note. If the chord is built from a C major scale like in my previous post and is a 1-3-5 chord it is a C major chord.

A lot of people have already heard that major chords often have a happier or positive quality and that minor chords have a more sad or apprehensive quality, but the reality is that this depends entirely on the context that this chord is played in. That’s for another lesson on chord progressions.

We can add more than 3 notes to a chord of course. Adam Neely on YouTube does a lot of really great music theory videos. He described the additional notes beyond the 3 note tritonal chord as adding additional flavor to that chord. So a if we stack another third on to our 1-3-5 chord we have a 1-3-5-7. This is called a 7th chord. Again depending on whether the 3 is flat or not this would either be a major 7th or a minor 7th. And it has a quality of being a little more interesting than the regular major or minor. You get a little more complexity in the sound because you are now getting the interaction between 1-3, 1-5, 1-7, 3-5, 3-7,and 5-7.

Chords with more than 3 notes are called extended chords. Makes sense when you think about how those additional tones change the way the basic triad sounds.

I didn’t mention this previously, but you can keep numbering above 7 as well. An 8 is the same as a 1, but one octave higher. A 9 is the same as a 2 but one octave higher and so on.

So we could keep stacking thirds past the 7th degree.
A 1-3-5-7-9 chord is called a major or minor 9th and is really complex. A whole other set of interactions on top of what we had in the 7th. These 9th chords are common in both electronic and jazz music.

Since a 9 and a 2 are really the same letter note but an octave apart a 1-3-5-7-9 chord is actually the same as a 1-2-3-5-7 chord! They will sound slightly different of course, but both the tonality (C,D,E,etc) and the quality (happy, sad, simple, complex, jazzy, etc) will be similar.

You can keep adding notes all the way up and down the scale degrees- 13th, 14th etc. but after the 7 you are really just either duplicating or filling notes in the 1 to 7 scale at higher (or lower) octaves.

You can add some additional flavor or seasoning in to change the quality of the chords by filling in some of the other gaps between these intervals. There are names for what happens if you add in a 4 or a flat 9 for example, but you could go on with that stuff for ever and I can’t keep them straight.

But this is one of the ways in which Auxy or really any electronic music tool excels. On a guitar you can only play 6 notes at the most at once (one for each string). On a piano you are limited to 10 fingers.

Who knows what the limit is on simultaneous tones in Auxy. Not only can you play many notes at once, you can also play many instruments at once. And with delays and extended reverb you can have (for example) a 9-11-13 chord trailing on still while you move into a 1-3-5-7 chord and the bass plays a 4th degree thus creating that massive sounding magical (or horrible depending on the context) Dminor13add4 chord.

And again you did it without even knowing the names of the notes go into the D minor scale. In this lesson you just had counting to thirteen!

Shepdoge March 30, 2018, 9:07am #19

Sorry mate but I’m going to have to pull you up on a few bits of incorrect information. Other than these issues, you’ve explained the basics in a relatively cohesive manner

Cheddr:

…C (the 1st degree) and F (the fifth degree) sound good together.

G is the fifth scale degree in the key of C major, not F, which is the fourth degree.

Cheddr:

To make a major scale minor the rule is to make the 3rd scale degree flat (lower pitched) by one half step.

You’ll also need to flatten the 6th and 7th degrees of the major scale to get the parallel minor. What you’ve described here is the melodic minor scale.

Cheddr:

Any three or more notes at the same time is called chord and a chord with three notes is called a tritonal chord.

You’ll hear chords with three different notes called “triads” more often. Chords with more than those three notes (not sure about seventh chords) are usually called extended chords (i.e. chords with added 9ths, 11ths, 13ths)

Cheddr:

…a 2-4-6 from the C major scale would be a D major and a 3-5-7 an E major.

In the C major scale, the 2-4-6 chord will be a minor chord. Why, you ask? The intervals, if we were to play a major scale, would be:

W-W-H-W-W-W-H
(or in C major)
C-D-E-F-G-A-B

H = halfstep/semitone/1 note on the chromatic scale
W = wholestep/tone/2 notes on the chromatic scale

Another example in G major:
G-A-B-C-D-E-F#

And F major:
F-G-A-Bb-C-D-E

The 2nd, 4th and 6th degrees have different spacing to the 1st, 3rd and 5th degrees. The minor [ii] chord in the key of C major (D minor) has only 3 halfsteps/semitones to get from D (the root note of the chord) to the next note in the scale, which is F (the third interval of the chord). That’s, as you said, the difference between a major chord and a minor chord; the third interval. Minor thirds = 3 semitones, and major thirds = 4 semitones

NotMiles:

reconstructed melodies

Not sure what you mean here.
If you’re not using the melodies as they appear in the source composition, then you won’t need to get a cover license.

If you’ve created something that ‘sounds like the melody from that game’, then the work could be regarded as being ‘heavily inspired’, maybe even ‘derivative’, but probably not technically an infringement.

 

The problem is that (imo) that my style varies a bit to much to keep it consistent on either account.

I have the same issue.
One solution is that you don’t necessarily ‘release’ everything you create. At least, not on Spotify, iTunes, etc…

Keep those ‘proper’ channels focused, using one or two distinct sounds.

I certainly won’t be releasing certain tracks that I had* up on SC.
(* I recently removed half of my SC tracks, removing anything that definitely didn’t fit with the Heathen AKA sound. I’ll be culling more soon as I tighten that account up.)

For instance, I’ve decided that I’ll focus my formal output on two, possibly three, distinct styles – though that ‘3rd’ stuff might be remade slightly and brought under the Heathen AKA banner.

An example of something that doesn’t readily fit would be my ‘Sketch 23’ piece; a brassy, ‘classical’ piece with a mellotron ending. As it stands, it doesn’t fit with Heathen AKA, but by remaking it with a more gritty, synthetic sound, it might make an interesting addition to the Heathen AKA body of work. (And I’d still enjoy the piece in the new form.)

Of course, we need to ask ourselves, is everything we create worthy of a proper release?

We’re on Spotify presumably because we want to take our music more seriously. Part of that is being more critical about the quality of the stuff we’re producing – and/or making sure that everything we do finish really is of the kind of quality that’s going to build an audience.

 
It’s important to understand that ‘Why do I create music?’ and ‘Why do I release music on Spotify, etc…?’ are two different questions.

If you create music for enjoyment, then anything goes.
But, if you’re releasing on Spotify, etc… to make it more serious, then some practical ‘rules’ are going to apply; rules that impact how best to build an audience – and establish yourself as a serious music maker.

The answers to both questions only need to align if you’re set on releasing everything you make on Spotify, …

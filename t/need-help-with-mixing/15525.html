EQ is going to be your best friend when it comes to mixing. High/lowpass, boosting areas you want heard and lowering harsh frequencies or areas you dont want to hear.

As for a way to keep your tracks at a more manageable volume, lower the volume of all new tracks you make so that the instrument sits at around -12db and so that the master sits below 0db. Then you can adjust the individual volumes to get the track sounding how you want, and at the end, put a limiter on the master to help bring the track‚Äôs volume back up for export. at least that‚Äôs what i try to do.

Db909 May 21, 2019, 8:30pm #12

All great suggestions here. I would also suggest playing around with mixing at a low volume, as low as you can go, so that you can really pick out the transients/loudest parts. With these sitting together right, things will sound great turned back up. You may have to occasionally turn it up and down to get a handle on the bass. Also you can play around with setting the device to mono audio in the accessibility settings, think of this as just another way of limiting your point of view to really focus in on how things are sitting together. Mixing is how you craft the perception of the audio, so it can pay to play with your own perception like this.

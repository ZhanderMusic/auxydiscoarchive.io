MR_ANDERSON February 8, 2018, 8:59pm #5

I have had this same question before! I will say right off the bat, that experimentation is a solid way to find good chords. The word “bittersweet”, for me, sort of implies tension and/or sadness. So, to start, pick a key signature that sounds sad (maybe F minor, B flat minor, or C minor, to name a few). Then, maybe you want some dissonance. Not all dissonance sounds good, but when used in the right context, notes outside of a basic triad can actually sound SUPER cool. So that’s my advice. Experiment with stuff, and try to incorporate notes from outside of the standard triad of a chord.

MR_ANDERSON February 8, 2018, 9:06pm #7

Using extra notes can be trick sometimes. To begin, you have to view the scale in the context of numbers. The root note is 1, the next one up is 2, then 3, and so on. So if I wanted to make a “bittersweet” chord using the root note, I would place a note on the 1, 3, 5, 7, and maybe even 9 (if you keep counting up). This makes the chord sound much more unique. Then, if you want to, you can rearrange the notes (except for the root note). If you keep the root note on the bottom of the chord, and rearrange the other notes, this also makes it sound nicer.

TheRealJFalc February 8, 2018, 9:06pm #8

It depends on what key your in, and what chord you’re leading to next. More often than not, adding a note “outside” a chord can actually change the chord into something else if it follows harmonic theory in a certain way. For example, a ii chord is only one note different than a IV chord. A ii7 chord can sound like a IVadd6 chord. It all depends on how you’re using it.

Jazz theory is fun!

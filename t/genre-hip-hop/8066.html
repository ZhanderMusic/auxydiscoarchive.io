akabillposters March 1, 2018, 1:07pm #5
Historically, breakbeat…

Historically, ‘break beats’ were used in early hip-hop for breakdancing. They were a component of hip-hop/breakdance music, rather than the genre itself.

‘Breakbeat’, as a genre, was an early '90s pre-cursor to Jungle and was traditionally revolved around mid-uptempo breaks, mixing in hardcore house vibes. Spiritually, it was a ‘rave’ vibe with heavy breakbeat drums.

A good example would be Manix or pretty much anything from early '90s Reinforced Records or Shut Up & Dance Records.

As it progressed, it gathered influences, splintering into Jungle, DnB, Hardcore (which melded with some techno influences), etc…

 
In more recent years, ‘Breakbeat’ is more widely used to refer to bass-heavy, heavy beat-driven electronic. The drum rhythms will still use similar patterns to trad breakbeat (i.e. patterns that originated in hip-hop), but the sampled beats have now mostly been replaced with programmed drums.
It’s also closer to mid-tempo and the low end of uptempo, compared to trad breakbeat. More innately ‘danceable’ as a genre than hip-hop.

 

As I see it (hear it), contemporary breakbeat is spiritually quite close to Big Beat.

Akin to a descendent of The Chemical Brothers’ heavier stuff mixed with some EDM and Dubstep composition and production touches. Certainly, energy-wise, Breakbeat is closer to EDM and Dubstep.

From the sound of it, Breakbeat artists tend to come mostly via the Electronic/EDM influence, rather than the Hip-Hop influence.

 
Hip-Hop is the precursor to Rap and still represents the ‘softer’ side of that genre (though the two are still often used synonymously).

Bands that still represent the Hip-Hop vibe would be Tribe Called Quest, through mainstream artists like Snoop Dogg, 50 Cent, through to Kendrick Lamar and even Cardi B.

…spinning off into things like Trap.

…though the Hip-Hop influence has spread and touched pretty much every genre, even tweeny pop like Ed Sheeran and Taylor Swift.

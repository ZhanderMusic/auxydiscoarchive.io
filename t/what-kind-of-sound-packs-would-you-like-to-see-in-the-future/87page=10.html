Re: the infrastructure to supply sounds to people…

They already have a soundpack store, albeit tiny, and, I’d presume, the artists producing the existing packs are being financially rewarded, so the base IAP mechanics are technically and/or principally covered in that direction.

The challenge would be widening the ‘inbound’ channel.

They could go the ‘halfway’ route, stay curated, but increase the number of packs sought, processed and made available. They could do this by publishing soundpack requirements/formats/etc and then allow open submissions for review and consideration.

Or they could go the wide open route and create a proper, open market, where soundpack creators can upload and sell, with the app having a proper store.

Obviously, the latter is a significantly more complex proposition, but maybe a natural evolution at some point down the line.

The ‘halfway’ route seems like a good, near-future evolution.

@InfinityBeatz

I dont think we need a whole bunch of random of the wall sounds everywhere…

What you mean is that you don’t need “a whole bunch of random of the wall sounds everywhere”, right?
Of course, you’d be free to not buy or use them.

What counts as ‘random, off-the-wall sounds’ to you, might be exactly what another person is looking for.
I’ve seen comments about ‘The Future’ soundpack being less usable. They’re certainly a little more ‘unsubtle’ than other packs. I personally think they’re an interesting set and already use them.
Conversely, I was lukewarm about the new Melodic Percussion set, and they’ll likely get less use than other sets. I personally wouldn’t have made them a priority, but it’s not my app and it’s not my audience/customer-base.

Simple ‘sort’ metrics, such as “popular soundpacks” tend to take care of quality control, as the less appealing ones sink to the bottom of a list instead of cluttering up the interface.
There are many ways to let users see what’s relevant to them.

And who knows, maybe it’s the quickest way to get the orchestral strings/percussion and fat, Moogy basses/leads that would likely be very popular.

 

…chance of losing money…

It’s an app business. There’s an inherent chance of losing money.

Becoming ‘middle-men’ resellers, taking a slice for the hosting and promotion, is actually a reasonable way to limit costs and still generate a low-maintenance income stream.

I don’t know the engine underneath Auxy or soundpacks, whether they’re sounds-based (potentially large audio files) or merely synth engine parameters (potentially tiny text files), but either way, we’re not talking about YouTube, with a constant deluge of content being added. The number of creators who will add soundpacks would be relatively low to begin with, so there’ll be time to respond to demand.

 

…there are literally only 2 people full time working on this app

 
That’s true — for now.

As demand grows or an audience emerges that points to additional revenue streams, then growing the team and exploring/exploiting the opportunity would be a business-like thing to do.

Of course, if it’s just a passion project, and the plan is to limit Auxy to whatever the current two-person team can achieve, then that would be a shame.

What better way to make a living and/or grow a business than through something you created through genuine interest and passion, which became successful beyond your expectations?

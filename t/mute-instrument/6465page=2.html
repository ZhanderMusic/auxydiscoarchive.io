akabillposters January 24, 2018, 1:12am #29

I’m not sure you’ve understood the scenario I described. You seem to be talking about static, steady sounds, that occupy a more consistent set of frequencies, while I’m actually talking about sounds that move around with modulation. i.e. LPF modulation is already beingused as part of the sound design.

e.g.
https://soundcloud.com/akasnd/passing-the-red-giant#t=0:50
https://soundcloud.com/akasnd/the-10-year-slump#t=3:55

If a sound has both low end, plus some mid-low warmth and just enough res to make it sizzle (e.g. fat Moog basses), using LPF and HPF to control amplitude will impact the shape of the waveform on the way down, ‘fading out’ frequencies from the ends first, moving inwards.

Same issue with band pass filtering. It necessarily effects the shape of the waveform, not just amplitude.

That why I say amplitude is the right tool for the job when you simply want to increase or reduce volume evenly - i.e. without impacting the shape of the waveform.

 

I am not sure resonance is being used correctly because in audio engineering it is used to refer to the boosted frequencies inside the filter cutoff (meaning the resonance can change but cannot be eliminated).

It’s possible.
Fwiw, I’m referring to the ‘sizzle’ that you get on those fat Moog basses I mentioned. On analogue synths, such as the Moog range, you’ll often see a res control.

@Zeph

But, as mentioned, there are common scenarios why volume automation is the right and only solution to amplitude control.

e.g…

Want to simply fade something in/out without effecting the character of the sound (the shape of the waveform)? LPF/HPF automation isn’t going to work.

Want to have an instrument higher/lower in the mix during a part of the track - without effecting the character of the sound? You’re going to need to use the volume automation feature.

 

The whole point of this tip is so that when you are trying to lower the volume of a specific instrument, the automation does not interfere and then turn the volume back up.

Sounds like a situation where creating a new volume automation pattern to combine the volume automations would be the solution.

Here’s a v rough example of what I mean…

image.png300×518 34.3 KB

Using volume automation to create a fade out, A4 and B4 is the same sound, just at different amplitudes, because you’re not altering any LPF/HPF settings or automations that may be in effect.

Using LPF/HPF to create a fade out, A4 and B4 are characteristically different sounds – ‘quieter’, but also different waveforms.
Here, you’re progressively moving the sound further away from its original waveform – which may be undesirable.

-

Basically, the OP is after a non-destructive (or least destructive) way to temporarily mute and then unmute instruments.

The instrument level mute/solo controls, mentioned earlier in this thread, are the ideal solution.

I know this is an old topic, a little unrelated, and it might already be common knowledge to some, but;
I just discovered that you can mute delay and reverb of an instrument and release when they overlap into the next scene where it’s not being used.
Volume and similar automations only work when it’s playing a loop. So, when you need to mute all aspects of an instrument from one scene to the next, you can use any (or create a throwaway) loop and have an automation with volume at 0.

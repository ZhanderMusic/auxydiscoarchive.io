I think the ‘so be it’ attitude would be wrong for a business seeking to grow audiences and revenue. You can’t (read: shouldn’t) be that passive about it.

I think it would ‘silly’ if Auxy weren’t actively using their SC channel to cast the best - and broadest - net. To maximise appeal (at least, in terms of genre relevance). But, that’s exactly what appears to have been happening, supported by statements Auxy devs have made that point to them not utilising it as a formal marketing channel (supporting marketing objectives), but a more personal, fun side-thing for Lenberg and Fredrik.

The point is that, even if the very best tracks produced organically amongst the user community were routinely of a single, certain genre, as the product owner, you’d presumably feel the need to actively seek out and introduce good music from other genres too, to show that the product wasn’t a ‘one trick pony’, and create interest amongst potential users who might not be persuaded by the app’s well established FB credentials alone.

If it was the case that all non-FB music simply wasn’t considered ‘good enough’, then, knowing how important it is to show the app’s broad genre potential and broaden its appeal, I’d look at ways of helping users improve their music writing and production techniques.

One form this could take is tutorial and inspiration content, aimed at improving music writing and production techniques.

Additionally, the company could consider encouraging (or even commissioning) ‘show-piece’ tracks, from better producers, in the genres that aren’t yet well represented organically in the top tier of quality tracks.

(People, especially at the non-pro end, often need help understanding the potential of something - and the relevance to them. I’d say that Auxy is primarily aimed at the non-pro end - those looking to get into music creation for the first time or those in the earlier stages of an interest in music creation. They may have trouble appreciating how Auxy can support their desire to create electro-pop/hardcore techno/ambient/whatever, if all they really hear (from the company’s marketing channels) is FB, for instance.

—

I don’t think this topic has ever actually been about users ‘aiming for gold stars’. If it was, we’d presumably see fewer complaints as people would simply switch to making the style of music that appeared most likely to get a repost. This issue has been about knowing whether or not the music of those producing in less popular genres is being noticed and fairly considered for reposts - and how personal genre preferences might be influencing reposts.

( I agree that ‘gold stars’ ideally shouldn’t be the driving force behind our music, but it’s easy to understand why they’re so desirable. Appreciation is appreciated, even by those who aren’t ‘serious’ about making music.
And for those who are more serious, without appreciation and promotion, an interest in making music more seriously will never get beyond a hobby. So, while reposts hopefully aren’t ‘the point’, they could represent a valuable early bump in exposure for those seeking to make music more seriously.

The largest part of getting there is improving one’s own writing and production, but also encouraging channels to be consider and appreciate one’s music.)

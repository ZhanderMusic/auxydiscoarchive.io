akabillposters September 25, 2017, 6:59am #24

I guess that’s fair enough. Content ID claims are a far more common way for copyright holders to deal with infringers on YT these days.

Howver, if you get even one copyright takedown, which is the other option for holders and comes with a channel strike, you may need to rethink things. It only takes three active* strikes for a channel to get terminated.

So, if you’re posting content as regularly as you’d need to, to establish an audience, and enough of that content is unlicensed, your approach may not work long-term.

* each strike expires after three months, afaik.

 
This approach reminds me of how a hip-hop producer call DJPain1 talks on his channel about (not) licensing samples.
His view is that you should use the samples without permission and if/when the holder comes knocking, let them have all the revenue.

If the track using the unlicensed samples blows up, becomes a hit and gets lots of attention - which is typically the point at which the holder comes knocking - even though you’ll make little/no money off that track, you’ve still benefitted by launching your name/career as a music producer.

Can’t say I’m a fan of the approach, but it’s increasingly popular these days - and it’s a ‘hustle’ that seems to work sometimes.

I’d guess that it’s often the same thinking behind unofficial/bootleg remixes.

So, being prepared to sacrifice your revenue to build a reputation that you can possibly leverage later for money and opportunities later.

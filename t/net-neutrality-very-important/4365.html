Warning, long post! TL;DR: It’s incredibly important.

iammane:

We wouldn’t have to pay for anything

Yes … and no. It would open the doors to service providers, for example, charging a premium to use any kind of video streaming. And they might, for example, charge YouTube and Vimeo (etc) a premium to be able to stream their video content across the ISP’s network. This will result in more advertising to end users to cover their costs, or even direct fees to the end user.

Some ISPs might decide they won’t allow online gameplay of any games that they deem to be “inappropriate”. So you might find that you can’t play GTA on your current ISP because they think it’s too violent, but you can play Second Life. So you switch ISPs only to find that Second Life isn’t allowed because they believe there is too much sexual content, but GTA is allowed.

Your ISP might be in bed with Google or even part-owned by Google, so Android updates might come down quick as lightning, while iPhone updates are throttled to a 2-day-download crawl. Or they might have a deal with Microsoft, so Skype might work great on your network but FaceTime may be crippled to the point of being unusable.

All of this would also stifle competition from smaller companies. If someone was to create a new video streaming service it probably wouldn’t be able to afford the fees to the ISPs to get them to allow their traffic on their network, while YouTube etc would happily be able to pay those fees. Or if your ISP is owned by Google, they might make the fees prohibitively high to those startups they feel threatened by.

More worryingly …

iammane:

I think it’s more a problem for freedom of speech

Yes … it would allow ISPs to pick and choose which sites and services they will allow on their networks. One company might decide they don’t like porn sites. Another might take exception to YouTube. Some might not like LiveLeaks, some might create their own biased competition to Wikipedia which they allow access to while blocking Wikipedia.

ISPs might make the sites of political groups that they dislike inaccessible, while allowing access to the sites of the group(s) they like. And/or political parties might lean on ISPs or bribe ISPs to promote their sites’ traffic across the network over the other groups’ traffic.

iammane:

I’ve been hearing about this for yearrrrrsssss now though. Like literally four or five years.

That’s because politicians keep trying to erode net neutrality laws but the people keep fighting them on it. Those politicians will keep trying to erode until they wear the people down and get it passed. We’ve got to stay vigilant because 95%+ of all politicians are crooked and in the back pocket of some big business that stands to make money from a change in the law.

A similar thing happened here in the UK with the net surveillance law. The government wanted to basically be able to monitor E V E R Y T H I N G that happens on the Internet in the UK, and even data that crosses the UK (as most traffic to/from the states from the EU goes directly through the UK). They tried for years to get that passed and it was rejected because it was a horrible abuse of power. But they wore everyone down and snuck it through parliament while everyone was distracted by the fallout from Brexit, and so now it’s law.

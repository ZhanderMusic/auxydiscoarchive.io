Stigmo July 12, 2017, 12:16pm #46

I usually just start by making a 4x4 beat, duplicate it and then mess around in the copy (changing the last bar so that it’s more interesting to listen to and not as repetitive). When you are satisfied, you can blend it together into an 8-bar loop, which shapes up your song a little more. Then you can copy this 8-bar loop change it up again, for example by adding different instruments or changing the melody to keep it interesting (but keep it related to the first 8-bar loop, since you want to have a coherent song). This way your ‘jingles’, as you call them, can form the basis of your song ;).
I usually work like this, since it works for me. I usually make house music though, so I don’t know if this is useful for you and the type of music you like to produce. I hope this helps you out a bit, good luck!

xavior July 12, 2017, 12:23pm #47

I usually have a plan for each song before I even start using the app: 1) Intended tone/mood of the track (usually consistent with other tracks in the same EP/album), 2) Subgenre I’m aiming for (i.e. if I know I want to create a moombahton track, I know exactly what drum pattern to use and that makes it easier to get the beat started), and lastly 3) I decided whether or not the track is intended to have vocals or not because that will change the outcome of the track’s construction.

Ultimately, bass is my most important factor in each song because it lays out the foundation of a track’s sound. It’s what listeners will thump or sway too. I tend to keep bass patterns simplistic (even if the drop is 100% consistent of bass) because that’s just my style, but I always stress the importance of a solid bassline in a track.

Hope this helps one way or another

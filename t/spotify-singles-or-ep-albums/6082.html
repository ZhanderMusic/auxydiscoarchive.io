akabillposters January 11, 2018, 8:48am #4

I’m with @NickElle on this one.
I’d get the good stuff out there as soon as it’s ready.

My own playlists contain many artists who only had a single track released on Spotify when I added them - and yet, that one track has been out there, getting them exposure and finding its way onto playlists - which is where people most likely encounter them.

I’d recommend checking out the LANDR Blog. It has lots of useful and insightful articles about promotion, including how to approach getting your music onto playlists.

https://blog.landr.com/?s=playlist

There’s also the ‘digital’ factor. EPs and albums have become less important in the age of digital streaming. They’re still great for deepening a fanbase, making fans of a track into fans of an artist - or for establishing credentials as a ‘similar artist’ through which many listeners discover artists.
But, it remains the case that music is increasingly discovered by mechanisms set up to promote single tracks.

One thing you can do is check through the releases of your favourite artist who had small beginnings, but who now get good play numbers.

Look for patterns.
Did they start with an EP? (Were there guest remixers that might have helped feed across new listeners)?
Or did they drop a series of singles and then an EP or album?
Was that EP/album new material or mostly a gathering of existing materials?

 
Re: Off-putting upload issues.

I’d suck up any First-World upload problems, rather make your future audience wait.

 
Bottom line, imo, don’t wait. Your starting strategy should be aimed at getting heard. That ain’t happening while you’re holding back on your first releases.

NickElle January 12, 2018, 5:43pm #6

I don’t know if it’s wise to release them on SC before selling your projects.
Releasing them for free in SC then putting out for money kinda weakens the punch of your music’s impact.
Doing that is called a “rerelease.” It is helpful when you want to give new life into a track that is SUPER old (like years and years old), but rereleasing a track that is new (even one that is a year old) will make your brand look like a spammer.

I would suggest planning on which singles, EPs, LPs to release for profit beforehand so your projects don’t end up shooting themselves in the foot.

Here’s an example of what I mean by planning: Say you’ve got 2 tracks you’re working on. One is a collab track that you are on, and the other is your own original music.
You like the collab, but it’s not really your style, because it’s a combination of your’s and someone else’s, and the solo track is your “baby.” You slave over that track because you want it to be perfect.
